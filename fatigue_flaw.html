Fatigue + Flaw Example

Example 7-11 — Design of a Fatigue Resistant Plate

Problem
A high-strength steel plate has a plane-strain fracture toughness of \(K_{IC} = 80\; MPa\sqrt{m}\). The plate is alternately loaded in tension to \(500\,MPa\) and compression to \(60\,MPa\).
The plate must survive for 10 years with the stress applied once every five minutes. Assume a geometry factor \(f = 1.0\) for all flaws.
Design a manufacturing and inspection procedure that ensures the component will survive the required lifetime.
Step 1 — Determine the Critical Crack Length
Failure occurs when the stress intensity factor reaches the fracture toughness:
\( K_{IC} = f\sigma\sqrt{\pi a_c} \)
Substituting the given values:
\( 80 = (1.0)(500)\sqrt{\pi a_c} \)
Solving for the critical crack size:
\( a_c = 0.0081\, m = 8.1\, mm \)
Step 2 — Determine Stress Range
Cracks do not propagate during compression, therefore the minimum stress used in fatigue calculations is zero.
\( \Delta\sigma = \sigma_{max} - \sigma_{min} \)
\( \Delta\sigma = 500 - 0 = 500\, MPa \)
Step 3 — Determine Required Number of Cycles
\( N = (1 \; cycle/5\,min)(60\,min/hr)(24\,hr/day)(365\,day/yr)(10\,yr) \)
\( N = 1{,}051{,}200 \; cycles \)
Step 4 — Crack Growth Using Paris Law
Fatigue crack growth follows Paris Law:
\( \frac{da}{dN} = C(\Delta K)^n \)
where
\( \Delta K = f\Delta\sigma\sqrt{\pi a} \)
Using constants from Figure 7-20:
\( C = 1.62\times10^{-12}, \quad n = 3.2 \)
Integrating the crack growth equation between the initial crack size \(a_i\) and the critical crack size \(a_c\) gives:
\( N = \frac{2(a_c^{(2-n)/2} - a_i^{(2-n)/2})} {(2-n)C(f\Delta\sigma)^n\pi^{n/2}} \)
Substituting values and solving for the allowable initial crack size:
\( a_i = 1.82\times10^{-6} m \)
\( a_i = 0.00182\, mm \)
For internal flaws:
\( 2a_i = 0.00364\, mm \)
Manufacturing must limit surface flaws to: \( a_i < 0.00182\, mm \) Internal flaws must satisfy: \( 2a_i < 0.00364\, mm \)
Engineering Interpretation
The manufacturing process must produce extremely small flaws. In addition, nondestructive inspection techniques must be capable of detecting cracks approaching this size to ensure long-term fatigue reliability of the component.
